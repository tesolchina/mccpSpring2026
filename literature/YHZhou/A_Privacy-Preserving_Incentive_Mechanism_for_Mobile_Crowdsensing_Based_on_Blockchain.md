# A Privacy-Preserving Incentive Mechanism for Mobile Crowdsensing Based on Blockchain
**Authors:** Fei Tong , Member, IEEE, Yuanhang Zhou , Kaiming Wang , Guang Cheng , Member, IEEE,

## Abstract

Abstract‚ÄîMobile crowdsensing (MCS) is an efficient approach for large-scale sensing data collection by leveraging the mobility and capability of mobile devices. To avoid the weaknesses of traditional centralized crowdsensing systems, blockchain has been introduced to secure the process of MCS. This article studies a location-aware scenario, where privacy of users are protected in a blockchain- based MCS system, and formulates an optimization problem to maximize the coverage given a budget based on reverse auction. An incentive mechanism named MMCB is further proposed and implemented as smart contracts in blockchain to solve the problem. We demonstrate that the mechanism achieves a set of desirable properties, including computation efficiency, individual rationality, truthfulness, budget feasibility, approximation, and privacy preservation. To protect the identity privacy of workers and obtain anonymity, a linkable ring signature is employed in smart contracts. In addition, a Pedersen commitment is utilized for protecting workers‚Äô bid profile and the submitted sensing data is encrypted and only accessible to the requester. We implement a prototype system based on the Hyperledger Fabric platform, and the evaluation results show that our privacy-preserving incentive mechanism architecture improves 36.2% coverage and reduces 53.1% payment with better security level compared to the stateof-the-art schemes.

## RELATED WORK

### Privacy and Security in Traditional Crowdsensing

The privacy and security issue has been one of the research hotspots in crowdsensing [21]. Privacy leakage mostly occurs when workers submit data with private information like location in LBS, decreasing workers‚Äô enthusiasm for participation. Besides, evil-minded workers may submit malicious data to wreck the crowdsensing system. In traditional crowdsensing, Yang et al. [22] utilized kanonymity to incentivize participation in an anonymity set. They consider the case where different users have different privacy requirements. Restuccia et al. [23] proposed a scalable secure trust-based framework FIDES based on mobile security agents and J√É¬∏sang‚Äôs trust model with three kinds of attack models. Li et al. [24] designed a scheme realized by trusted third party and a scheme combined of blind signature and commitment techniques to incentivize workers with privacy preservation. However, existing solutions either rely on centralized trusted party or consume large resources for privacy preserving. 
### Privacy Preservation in Blockchain

Blockchain provides a strongly secure environment for various applications by decentralization. However, due to the openness of blockchain, transactions in plaintext is available to all users in the system, thus privacy issue remains to be unsolved for blockchain-based systems. There have been many information concealment methods based on cryptography in blockchain, such as zero-knowledge proof [25], homomorphic encryption [26], secure multi-party computation [27], commitment scheme [28], attribute-based encryption [29] and differential privacy [30]. Some methods are widely applied in different fields. However, for crowdsensing, most schemes are not suitable for usage without adequate modification because of the limited computation capability of mobile devices and privacy requirements. 
### Incentive Mechanisms in Blockchain-Based Crowdsensing

Incentive mechanism plays a significant role in crowdsensing for improving participation and quality of sensed data. Research on incentive mechanism design is essential in both traditional and blockchain-based crowdsensing. There have been a number of researches on incentive mechanism design in crowdsensing using blockchain. [4] proposes a privacy-preserving incentive mechanism for vehicular crowdsensing based on budget-limited reverse auction using zeroknowledge proof and Pedersen commitment. However, the incentive mechanism can neither select workers according to their contribution nor obtain truthfulness. [7] combines a qualitybased incentive mechanism with k-anonymity privacy protection, ignoring the importance of information concealment. Both [4] and [7] consider the incentive mechanism design and privacy preservation, but the proposed incentive mechanisms lack an effective worker selection algorithm, and the privacy-preserving mechanisms are incomplete. ### Adversary Model and Assumptions

The adversary model and security assumptions considered in this article are shown as below. a) Curious requesters: This kind of users will honestly follow our defined protocols, but they are curious about the sensitive information in crowdsensing system, including the identity of the owner of a bid profile, the identity of winners, and the submitted data. They will submit true sensing tasks with a real budget, honestly acquire the submitted sensing data and evaluate such data as normal or malicious. However, they may spy on the transactions in blockchain and try to learn useful information. b) Malicious workers: Malicious workers tend to obtain more payment through misbehaviors like false information reporting, breaking the protocols, and so on. They may submit false bid profile in the bidding phase to gain advantage. They may submit useless data or even do not complete the submission and obtain a payment. They are also interested in the true identity of honest workers and the true information of sensing data. c) Malicious miners: Malicious miners try to obtain virtual coins aside from their jobs. They may violate the designed protocols and collude with other entities to break the normal execution of smart contracts in blockchain. 1) Sufficient bidders: It is assumed that the number of bidders is large enough to ensure that the incentive mechanism can normally select winners and computes payments from these bidders. 2) Majority honest security: Our crowdsensing system largely relies on a secure blockchain. We assume that the blockchain is secure, and attackers cannot control the majority power of blockchain. 3) Cryptographic methods security: We assume that all adopted cryptographic methods are secure. Detailedly, Ellipticcurve Cryptography is secured that a ciphertext encrypted with a public key can only be decrypted with the corresponding private key. Such an assumption is based on the NP hardness of Elliptic Curve Discrete Logarithm Problem. The adopted hash functions are secure, i.e., these functions satisfy preimage resistance, second-preimage resistance, and collision resistance [41]. The symmetric encryption method is secure, so that a ciphertext encrypted with a key can only be decrypted with the exact same key. 4) Ledger system security: We assume that a secure ledger system exists in the applied blockchain to support the assert transfer between valid users with valid keys. Such a ledger system adopts a kind of virtual coin that can be converted to currency.

### Privacy Goals

a) Bid profile protection: A worker‚Äôs bidding price should be concealed in the bidding phase to ensure the fairness of the auction-based incentive mechanism. If others‚Äô bidding prices are open, a subsequent worker will gain advantages in the auction by adjusting its bidding price according to others‚Äô prices. Besides, the information in the bid profile (i.e., bidding price, task set and location) is private, which should be concealed or unlinkable to the owner to ensure the privacy of workers. b) Anonymity: The identities of workers should be protected. No one can identify the real identities through the crowdsensing procedure. Specially, even the requester cannot match the corresponding worker with the workers‚Äô submitted bid profile or sensing data. c) Data submission security: The submitted sensing data of workers should be encrypted and only acquirable to the corresponding requester. Besides, due to the limit of the storage size and cost, sensing data may be inappropriate to be stored in the blockchain. While in Section V, we further design the detailed protocols for blockchain-based crowdsensing, in which the incentive mechanism is integrated into with privacy preservation.

### Bidding Phase

After registration, a worker can bid on the blockchain. Considering a worker i who is interested in several crowdsensing tasks combining a task set Ti , he/she can invoke the Bidding Contract to bid for tasks. The bid of worker i is formed as Œ∏i = {Li , Ti , bi }. All three elements in the triple are private, which should be protected from both malicious users and curious users in blockchain. Instead of providing the precise location, i only submits its located area numbers denoted by Li . Ti includes several task IDs indicating workers‚Äô intention. bi is a bidding price. Therefore, all three elements can be in the form of numbers. To conceal the real value of the three elements, Pedersen commitment is adopted. For a value v to be concealed, a commitment C(r, v) = rG + vH is calculated, where r is a blind factor, and G and H are two base points. The commitments are submitted instead of true values when bidding. The commitments will not be revealed unless the bidding phase ends. That it to say, because of the unconditionally hiding property of Pedersen commitment, there is no way for an observer to learn any useful information about the true value from a commitment, ensuring the fairness of the bidding phase. By introducing the linkable ring signature in the bidding phase, all users including the requester cannot figure out the identity of the bidder from the ring. Even though a malicious requester succeeds in obtaining the bid information of an anonymous user, he/she cannot match the information with a pseudonym. The ring signature satisfies the following properties, which ensures the anonymity theoretically. Unconditional anonymity: Attackers cannot find the true signer among the ring. The possiblity of finding the true signer is not beyond 1/n, where n is the ring size. Unforgeability: A user who is not a ring member cannot generate a valid ring signature in a formed ring. Similarly, when submitting the sensed data, the attached ring signature prevents the observer from finding the true owner of the submitted data. Besides, the linkability of the ring signature ensures that a worker can only bid once in the bidding phase, but the worker can be linked in the data submission phase with the anonymous identity in the bidding phase. Data Submission Security: In our crowdsensing system, the data are saved in IPFS to save the storage of the blockchain, and the IPFS does not have to be trustable. With the digest and address written on the blockchain, the requester can find the corresponding data and check the integrity of the data. The data is encrypted with a secure shared key computed from a generated one-time key that can only be obtained by the requester. Since the generated key is for one-time use, the encrypted data cannot be decrypted by anyone else except the requester. With the ring signature, data submission transaction can be verified anonymously. Since the submitted data will be checked for normality, where workers with poor-quality data will be punished and obtain no payment, malicious workers are unable to get free-riding payments unless they responsibly complete the sensing job. Incentive Mechanism Security: The incentive mechanism in the crowdsensing system is written as a contract and cannot be modified, while the detailed algorithm is public. The input includes the worker set, the bid profile and some parameters. The (anonymous) worker set and bid profile come from the Reveal Bid Contract, and the parameters are given by the requester, or follows the default setting. The execution of the incentive mechanism is automatic and cannot be disturbed, ensuring the safe run of the incentive mechanism. Since the proposed incentive mechanism is individually rational and truthful, the proposed incentive mechanism architecture is fair for all workers to bid. The output is written on the blockchain directly which cannot be revised by anyone else, ensuring the impartiality of worker recruitment. ## CONCLUSION

In this article, we proposed a privacy-preserving incentive mechanism architecture for mobile crowdsensing based on blockchain. First, we studied a location-aware mobile crowdsensing system and formulated the Maximizing Coverage under Budget constraint problem. By analyzing the difficulty of such a 5083 problem, an incentive mechanism MMCB is proposed, which satisfies a set of desirable properties, including computation efficiency, individual rationality, truthfulness, budget feasibility and constant approximation. To meet the privacy and security requirements, the privacy-preserving incentive mechanism architecture is proposed with each phase detailed. Specifically, a linkable ring signature is adopted to obtain anonymity. Pedersen commitment is used for concealing the workers‚Äô bids. Data submission is secured through storage on IPFS. We evaluated the performance of the proposed scheme based on the Hyperledger Fabric platform, and the obtained results show that the computation cost is acceptable and the performance of designed mechanism show great superiority on coverage function.
